Chess Royal

Chess has its origins in ancient India, where it was known as Chaturanga. This early form of chess emerged around the 6th century AD and was played on an 8x8 board.

The name "Chaturanga" means "four divisions of the military," referring to the infantry, cavalry, elephants, and chariots, which evolved into the modern chess pieces: pawn, knight, bishop, and rook.

The game spread to Persia, where it became known as Shatranj, and later reached Europe, evolving into modern chess.

Chess was highly regarded in medieval courts and was often used as a strategic tool to teach military tactics.

During the Renaissance, chess evolved further with new rules, such as the powerful movement of the queen and the standardization of the game board.

By the 19th century, chess clubs and competitive tournaments became popular, leading to the establishment of formal chess organizations.

The first official World Chess Championship took place in 1886, marking a new era of professional chess.

In the 20th century, advancements in chess theory and the rise of grandmasters contributed to the game's global popularity.

Computers revolutionized chess with AI-driven analysis, culminating in IBM's Deep Blue defeating Garry Kasparov in 1997.

Online chess platforms have further increased the accessibility and reach of the game, allowing players worldwide to compete in real-time.

Today, chess continues to thrive as an intellectual sport, blending historical traditions with modern technology.
